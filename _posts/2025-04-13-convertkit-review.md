## What We Didn't Like

### Visual Automations Lag Behind Competition

If you're used to ActiveCampaign, Mailchimp's automation builder, or Klaviyo, ConvertKit's feels basic. You can't build multi-branch logic visually — it relies more on rules and triggers than a canvas-style workflow builder.

For simple sequences (welcome → tag → send product pitch), it's fine. For complex customer journey mapping, you'll hit limitations fast.

Our workaround: we use ConvertKit for the email delivery and subscriber management, but map complex automations in Notion first, then translate to ConvertKit's rules-based system.

### Free Plan Is Really Limited

ConvertKit's free plan allows 0-100 subscribers with unlimited emails. That's genuinely useful for testing your opt-in offers.

But the moment you hit 100 subscribers, you're on a paid plan at $29/mo. No free plan for small creators who grow beyond 100 subscribers like Mailchimp offers.

For serious affiliate marketers, this is fine — but it's not the friendliest entry point for beginners.

### No A/B Testing on Subject Lines (Free vs. Pro)

A/B testing is available on Pro plans, but the testing options are more limited than competitors. You can test subject lines and send times, but not content or CTA testing.

This is a minor complaint — we don't A/B test subject lines frequently anyway — but it's worth noting if you're optimizing for maximum open rates.
